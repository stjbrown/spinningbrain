# YC RFS: Company Brain

Source: https://www.ycombinator.com/rfs  
Section: Summer 2026, "Company Brain" by Tom Blomfield  
Captured: 2026-05-27

## Summary

YC's Company Brain request argues that the next blocker for AI automation is not model capability, but company-specific domain knowledge. Companies run on scattered operational know-how: Slack threads, old emails, support tickets, databases, docs, and tacit memory in people's heads. Humans can navigate that mess imperfectly because they remember where things are and how exceptions work. Agents cannot.

The requested primitive is a system that extracts knowledge from fragmented sources, structures it, keeps it current, and makes it executable by AI systems. YC explicitly frames this as more than company search or a chatbot over docs. The target is a living operational map of how the company works.

One useful phrase from the RFS: "the missing layer between raw company data and reliable AI automation."

## Fit with hosted LLM Wiki

The hosted LLM Wiki idea maps closely to this request if the product is framed as a maintenance-first company knowledge layer rather than a generic wiki.

Strong overlaps:

- Shared company brain that humans and agents can both read.
- Continuous ingestion from fragmented company systems.
- Structured pages that compile operational knowledge instead of just retrieving raw chunks.
- Agent-facing access through MCP, API, or generated skills files.
- Human-facing UI for browsing, reviewing, and editing knowledge.
- Maintenance loop that detects stale, contradictory, duplicated, or missing knowledge.

The RFS strengthens the case that the product should focus on operational knowledge, not just document search. Examples include refund policies, pricing exceptions, incident response, onboarding paths, support escalations, customer-specific edge cases, and engineering release practices.

## Product Implications

The core product should probably be a governed knowledge compiler:

- Connectors ingest sources from Slack, GitHub, Linear/Jira, Google Drive, Notion/Confluence, support tools, CRM, meeting notes, and databases.
- The system proposes wiki updates when source material changes.
- Humans approve, reject, or edit proposed changes before they become canonical.
- Every page carries provenance, freshness, owners, version history, and access rules.
- Agents retrieve only the knowledge allowed by the user or agent identity invoking them.
- The system can generate agent-ready context: MCP tools, API responses, workflow instructions, or skills files.

## Differentiation

This should not be positioned as "AI search for your docs." That category is already crowded.

More defensible positioning:

> A permissioned company knowledge compiler that turns scattered work artifacts into current, reviewable, agent-readable operating knowledge.

Key differentiators to explore:

- Suggest-changes workflow rather than automatic silent mutation.
- Page-level and claim-level provenance.
- Freshness and drift detection.
- Knowledge owner assignment and review queues.
- Versioned operational playbooks.
- Agent-safe permission enforcement.
- Exportable Markdown or git-backed storage for trust and portability.
- MCP-first integration so any agent can query the company brain.

## Risks

- Glean, Notion, Atlassian Rovo, Guru, Slite, and Document360 already cover pieces of this market.
- The wedge needs to be sharper than "better enterprise search."
- Connector quality and permission correctness are existential requirements.
- If generated pages become stale or unaudited, the product will amplify bad context.
- Enterprise buyers will require SSO, SCIM, RBAC, audit logs, retention controls, and security review early.

## Takeaway

The YC RFS is strong validation for the hosted LLM Wiki direction, but it also raises the bar. The opportunity is not merely hosting Markdown pages generated by an LLM. The opportunity is building the trusted maintenance and governance layer that lets company knowledge stay current enough for agents to act on it.
